Parmenides

Heidegger analyzes Parmenides’ conception of being as an absolute, eternal, unchanging, and unitary reality. For Parmenides, being is indivisible and unchanging, while multiplicity and change are merely sensory illusions. Heidegger also discusses Parmenides’ concept of nonbeing as nonexistent.

Heidegger sees Parmenides’ significance in being the first to separate the concepts of absolute being and changing phenomena, paving the way for philosophical contemplation on the nature of being.

Through this work, Heidegger provides a profound hermeneutic reading of Parmenides’ metaphysical ideas that established a prominent philosophical strand in Greek thought.
