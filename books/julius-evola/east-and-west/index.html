East and West

Julius Evola

philosophy politics

“a study that examines the fundamental differences between Eastern and Western civilizations, particularly in terms of spirituality, metaphysics, and social structures”

view in arabic download section

In “East and West,” Julius Evola explores the differences between Eastern and Western civilizations. He argues that while both have declined from their traditional roots, Eastern traditions have maintained a spiritual character lacking in the rationalist West.

According to Evola, the core features of the Eastern worldview are contemplation, detachment, hierarchy, and an acceptance of the world’s mystery. This contrasts with the West’s emphasis on action, domination over nature, egalitarianism, and reliance on empirical reason. Eastern thought seeks inner wisdom, while Western thought aims to manipulate external reality.

Evola believes that early Eastern and Western societies shared a common Traditional foundation focused on the sacred. Over time the West lost this link as secularism, materialism and individualism took hold. While the East was not immune to modern decline, its indigenous traditions–like Hinduism, Taoism and Buddhism–retained an orientation toward transcendence that provides a connection to Tradition.

Evola admires the ascetic, monarchic, and contemplative aspects of ancient Eastern principles. He considers the East’s acceptance of hierarchy and rejection of modern rationalism as superior to Western democratic and egalitarian pretensions. Yet he also sees value in aspects of the Western outlook, like its focus on building civilization.

Ultimately Evola argues that for the modern world to revive its connection to Tradition, Eastern and Western approaches must synthesize. He attempts to highlight the strengths and weaknesses of both to help guide their convergence in a renewed Traditional civilization.
