Meditations on the Peaks

In Meditations on the Peaks, Julius Evola uses mountain climbing as a metaphor for the spiritual path according to the Traditionalist worldview. He argues that ascending a mountain requires qualities that parallel the inner journey to transcendence.

Evola sees climbing as a heroic act representing the Traditional virtues of courage, asceticism, and mastering the limitations of the material realm. Reaching a mountain’s summit provides an elevated vantage point above mundane affairs, reflecting the metaphysical nature of spiritual liberation.

According to Evola, the climber must face peril, hardship and the possibility of death stoically, with discipline and detachment. This mirrors the Traditional path of esoteric self-transformation through rigorous contemplation and self-mastery.

Evola relates mountaineering to principles like the “absolute individual,” Going beyond rationalism and attaining a transcendent awareness. The climb thus epitomizes the Traditionalist goal of breaking free from the constraints of physical existence.

Through its evocative metaphor, Meditations on the Peaks provides vivid insight into Evola’s philosophy of aligning oneself with impersonal, transcendent Truth through courage and inner conquest.
