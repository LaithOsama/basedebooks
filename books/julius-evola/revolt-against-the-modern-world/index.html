Revolt Against the Modern World

In “Revolt Against the Modern World,” Julius Evola outlines his philosophy of Traditionalism and opposition to modernity. He argues that the modern world has lost connection to the transcendent foundations of ancient traditions and cultures.

Evola believes that in ancient, traditional societies - such as in Hindu India, pre-Christian Europe, and the Roman Empire - individuals identified themselves with their role in an organic social hierarchy. Authority came from spiritual truths that ordered civilization and gave meaning to life. The rise of ideas like individualism, egalitarianism, and rationalism in the modern West have undermined this traditional social order.

According to Evola, the core features of the modern world include the rule of money-oriented economies, the mechanization and industrialization of life, democracy and mass society, and the predominance of rationalism and materialism. He sees these trends as indicative of a regressive historical process where the sacred truths at the heart of tradition have been lost.

Evola advocates rebelling against the modern world by embracing Tradition - timeless and transcendent principles deriving from a primordial heroic age. He draws from myth, religion, and philosophy to outline an idealized Traditional society with rigorous hierarchy, ascetic warrior values, and belief in mystical truths rather than rationality and science.

While controversial, Evola puts forth a trenchant criticism of modernity from an anti-egalitarian traditionalist perspective. He highlights the desire for meaning, heroism, and connection to the sacred that he sees modern civilization as lacking. His revolt is a call to reclaim the wisdom of past ages.
