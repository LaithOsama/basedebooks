The Crisis of the Modern World

In his book The Crisis of the Modern World, traditionalist thinker René Guénon presents a scathing criticism of modern Western civilization from a traditionalist lens, warning of the grave deviations that have affected contemporary Western thought.

Guénon argues that the root cause of the crisis lies in the abandonment of metaphysical principles and the dominance of materialism. He unveils the consequences of a society adrift from its spiritual moorings, as scientific rationalism and industrial progress lead to a disconnect between the individual and the higher realms of existence.

The book delves into the destructive impact of individualism, secularism, and relativism, which erode the spiritual foundations of civilizations. Guénon contrasts the modern world’s obsession with quantity and superficiality with the timeless wisdom of traditional cultures that upheld qualitative values and a deeper connection to the metaphysical realm.

Guénon’s treatise goes beyond mere critique; it offers an alternative perspective rooted in the perennial wisdom found in various spiritual traditions. He advocates for a return to traditional metaphysical principles as a means to restore harmony and coherence to a world in crisis, emphasizing the importance of spiritual insight and the recognition of higher realities.

Guénon sees the prime traits of modern Western civilization as extreme materialism and rationalism, blind faith in technological progress, and severance from the spiritual and traditional principles that distinguished ancient civilizations.

Guénon attributes the causes of this crisis to the rise of individualism and subjectivism in the modern era, and the loss of connection with perennial traditional knowledge that provided humans access to the Absolute. Thus, Guénon calls for the imperative return to fundamental spiritual principles to renew Western thought and save it from deviation.

“The Crisis of the Modern World” serves as a wake-up call to humanity, urging individuals to seek higher truths and resist the dehumanizing forces of modernity. Guénon’s profound analysis and his plea for a revival of metaphysical understanding offer a path towards restoring meaning and purpose in an age plagued by materialism and superficiality.

Despite his pessimism, Guénon raises in this book important warnings against the perils of severing from tradition and blindly indulging material progress without awareness of its nature and consequences.
