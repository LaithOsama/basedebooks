The King of The World

In The King of the World, René Guénon elucidates the metaphysical symbolism of the “King of the World” found in various traditional cultures. He argues this universal symbol points to a sacred spiritual authority governing terrestrial affairs from a transcendent realm.

Guénon finds the “King of the World” motif across traditions - in accounts like the Egyptian “Ruler of the Gods,” the Hindu Manu, and legends of the holy city Agarttha. This ubiquitous symbol expresses an idea of divine kingship embodying a sacred order on Earth.

According to Guénon, the “King of the World” represents the pole or axis linking the spiritual and material planes. He rules by divine right over temporalEarthly powers derive from this supreme spiritual authority governing material manifestation from beyond terrestrial existence.

Guénon relates the symbol to the metaphysical constitution of society with the priestly spiritual authority guiding the worldly temporal power. The disappearance of the “King of the World” in modern times reflects the severing from metaphysical principles.

With its exposition of this fundamental symbol, The King of the World exemplifies Guénon’s examination of tradition for glimpses into metaphysical truths within diverse cultures.
