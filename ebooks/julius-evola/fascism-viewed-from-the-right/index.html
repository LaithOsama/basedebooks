Fascism Viewed from the Right

Julius Evola

politics philosophy history

In this work, Evola provides a right-wing critique of Fascism focused on its implementation in Nazi Germany. He argues that despite positive tendencies, the Third Reich compromised its Traditionalist potential by retaining modernist influences.

Evola praises the Fascist rejection of bourgeois values, individualism and egalitarianism. However, he believes National Socialism’s emphasis on materialism, technology and mass mobilization diluted its transcendent possibilities.

According to Evola, the Third Reich lacked a truly radical antimodernist spirit. Its biological racism and paganism remained constrained by modern rationalism rather than fully embracing metaphysical roots. Nazi ideology retained humanist and idealist elements at odds with Traditionalism.

While acknowledging positive reforms, Evola ultimately sees the Third Reich as failing to institute an anti-modern, hierarchical order guided by an aristocracy of spirituality. Its compromises with modernity prevented Fascism from becoming the unambiguous counter-revolution Evola desired.

Despite some shared views, “Fascism Viewed from the Right” distinguishes Evola from Fascists by highlighting his uncompromising adherence to an essentialized spiritual Tradition.
