Ride the Tiger: A Survival Manual for the Aristocrats of the Soul

Julius Evola

philosophy spirituality

“explores the crisis of modernity and the challenges individuals face in finding meaning and purpose in a world characterized by spiritual emptiness and materialistic pursuits. Evola proposes a path of transcendence through self-mastery, advocating for a radical transformation of consciousness and a detachment from the illusions of modern society.”

In Ride the Tiger, Julius Evola outlines his views on the spiritual trajectory of modern Western civilization. He argues that the West has declined from a time of strong values and heroism to a period of dissolution, decadence, and nihilism. Evola refers to this as the “Dark Age,” where traditional social structures have broken down, moral relativism pervades, and individualism reigns supreme.

According to Evola, the predominant worldview in the modern West is humanism and materialism. Spiritual values have been replaced by economic gain, hedonism, and scientism. As a result, society lacks any transcendent orientation and people feel rootless and adrift. This has led to a kind of existential emptiness and inner dissociation.

In this environment, Evola believes there are essentially two options: active nihilism or detached witnessing. The former seeks to destroy corrupt structures and bring about a cleansing renewal. Evola sees this as futile in the long run. He advocates the latter path of apoliteia or “stepping back” to protect one’s inner transcendence. This involves a return to Tradition while refusing to actively participate in modern decadence.

Evola argues that rather than vainly trying to change the dominant order, it is better to cultivate an inner orientation towards traditionalism, spirituality, and transcendence. He draws on various philosophies like Taoism, Buddhism, and Stoicism to outline this path of detachment, contemplation, and self-mastery. This is how one can metaphorically “ride the tiger” and uphold civilization’s higher values even in the midst of its decline.

While controversial, Evola’s critique highlights the inner struggle facing modern individuals caught between nihilistic materialism and the desire for spiritual meaning in their lives. His call for detachment serves as a reflection on how traditionalists might preserve their beliefs as the world changes around them.
